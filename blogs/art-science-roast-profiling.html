ROASTING SCIENCE

The Art and Science of Roast Profiling

Discover how water temperature, grind size, and flow rate interact to create a balanced and flavorful cup. We break down the science behind achieving the perfect extraction every time.

The pour-over method is celebrated for its ability to produce a clean, clear, and nuanced cup of coffee. Unlike immersion methods like a French press, the constant flow of fresh water over the coffee grounds allows for a more controlled extraction. But what are the variables that make or break a great pour-over?

1. Water Temperature: The Energy Variable

Water temperature acts as the primary catalyst in coffee extraction. Too hot (above 205°F or 96°C), and you risk scorching the grounds, resulting in a bitter, astringent taste. Too cool (below 195°F or 90°C), and you won't extract enough of the desirable flavor compounds, leaving the coffee tasting sour and underdeveloped. The sweet spot, or the 'ground state' for most beans, lies between 195-205°F (90-96°C).

"Think of temperature as the energy you're giving the water molecules. More energy means a more aggressive extraction."

2. Grind Size: The Surface Area Problem

The size of your coffee grounds determines the total surface area available for extraction. A finer grind means more surface area, leading to a faster extraction. A coarser grind means less surface area and a slower extraction. For pour-over, a medium-fine grind, resembling table salt, is the ideal starting point. If your coffee tastes too bitter, try a coarser grind. If it's sour, go finer.

Consistency is key. A high-quality burr grinder will produce uniform particles, ensuring an even extraction and a more balanced cup.
